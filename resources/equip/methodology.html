Industry 4.0 and Productivity

Section 3 Methodology

This section introduces a number of indicators that will be used in the next sections to analyse the following strategic questions:

  • How successful is a country in the adoption of new technology - in general and in industry 4.0 in particular - in absolute and relative terms?
  • If a country has fallen behind in terms of adoption rate, what could constitute a sensible target for the country to catch up to?
  • How does a country’s industrial automation capacity respond to the newly emerging technologies?
  • How can one asses if a country’s industrial automation capacity has improved?
  • To what extent are manufacturing jobs in the country at risk of robot-based automation?
  • What are the productivity growth dynamics of a particular industry and what do they depend on?
  • Are positive productivity growth rates always good?
  • Are there industries that provide positive growth and employment prospective even during the most recent periods of global market integration and fast-paced technological advancements?
  • What is the exposure of a country to industries that have experienced positive employment, value added and productivity growth in the past?
  • How does the share of these industries evolve over time?

The main data sources are UN Comtrade (2019) for data on imports and exports of goods, UN INDSTAT (2019) for data on employment and value added on the industry level, the World Bank World Development Indicators (WDI 2019) for a country’s total employment, population and nominal gross domestic product and the International Federation of Robotics (IFR 2019) for the stock of industrial robots. For total manufacturing employment, data from the the International Labour Organisation (ILO 2019) is used. Alternatively, the reader could aggregate industry employment by UN INDSTAT (2019). This alternative is further discussed in the appendix.

In this section the index \(i\) refers to a country, \(j\) refers to an industry, \(k\) denotes a product or product group, while \(t\) denotes a time period (usually a year).

3.1 Adoption of new technologies

As technology is one main driver of economic development by increasing productivity, it is interesting to see at what stage of adoption a country currently is. If it is, for example, a leader in technology one would expect it to also be an exporter of the respective goods related to that technology. This is one way of determining if a country successfully adopted some technology, when it reached a point where it is even an exporter of it.

To become a technology leader, most countries first start by importing technology to adopt it and absorb it. This section begins by looking at import and export shares of the broadest category of relevant goods, capital goods and subsequently, analyses goods related to industry 4.0. That is, first goods related to all vintages of technology and then goods related to the latest wave of innovation.

Information on imports and exports is combined to determine the stage of technology adoption of a particular country.

3.1.1 Import share of capital goods

Starting with capital goods, how much capital goods are imported compared to a country’s total imports? This should be a first indication on the ability of a country to use these goods and the technology related to them.

Main question

  • Of all the goods imported by a country, how much are capital goods?

For a given country \(i\), product group \(k\) (capital goods) and period \(t\),

\[ Share~of~capital~goods~imports~in~total~imports_{it} = \frac{Imports_{ikt}}{Total~imports_{it}} \] which indicates how much of all goods imported are capital goods.

Data sources

The data sources for this indicator are provided in Table 3.1. Imports are taken from UN Comtrade (2019), whereas the database offers different classification of goods. ‘BEC’ is chosen as it is a classification that defines broad economic categories, such as capital goods (United Nations Trade Statistics 2003). For a more detailed description of the ‘BEC’ classification, see H in the appendix.

Table 3.1: Adoption of technologies embodied in capital goods
Indicator Variable Source
Share of capital goods imports in total imports Imports of capital goods of a country UN Comtrade (2019) BEC
Total imports of a country UN Comtrade (2019) BEC

3.1.2 Export share of capital goods

While imports can indicate the ability to use capital goods, exports show the extent to which a country is competitive enough to be able to export capital goods to the world market.

For a given country \(i\), product group \(k\) (capital goods) and period \(t\),

\[ Share~of~capital~goods~exports~in~total~exports_{it} = \frac{Exports_{ikt}}{Total~exports_{it}} \] which indicates how much of all goods exported are capital goods.

Data sources

The data sources for this indicator are provided in Table 3.2. Exports are again taken from UN Comtrade (2019) with ‘BEC’ classification.

3.1.3 Revealed comparative advantage of imports and exports of capital goods

As the simple share is a first very broad indicator of potential technology adoption by importing, one has to go further in order to establish if a country is adopting through imports or if it is already producing goods themselves. For this the question is if a country is importing more or less than the world average. The import share from above is related to the import share of capital goods in worldwide imports. This indicator is called ‘revealed comparative advantage (of imports)’ RCA. If this indicator is above one, the country imports relatively more capital goods than the average country.

Main question

  • Is a country importing more or less capital goods compared to the worldwide average?

\[ RCA~import~of~a~country_{ikt} = \frac{Imports_{ikt}}{Total~imports_{it}} / \frac{Imports_{wkt}}{Total~imports_{wt}} \]

\(Imports_{wkt}\) denotes world imports of capital goods, while \(Total~imports_{wt}\) represents total world imports of all goods.

Data sources

Table 3.3: RCA import of capital goods
Indicator Variable Source
RCA import of a country Imports of capital goods of a country UN Comtrade (2019) BEC
Total imports of a country UN Comtrade (2019) BEC
Total imports of capital goods in the world UN Comtrade (2019) BEC
Total imports from the world UN Comtrade (2019) BEC

3.1.4 Revealed comparative advantage of exports of capital goods

Once is is clear if a country imports more or less than the world average, this information can be combined with the export side. If, for example, it can be shown that a country imports less than the world average, but export more than the world average, the difference must be produced domestically. This would highlight that this country has adopted technology well enough to be able to export it.

Main question

  • Is a country exporting more or less capital goods compared to the worldwide average?

\[ RCA~export~of~a~country_{ikt} = \frac{Exports_{ikt}}{Total~exports_{it}} / \frac{Exports_{wkt}}{Total~exports_{wt}} \]

Data sources

The data sources are the same as above, UN Comtrade (2019) in the BEC classification.

Table 3.4: RCA export of capital goods
Indicator Variable Source
RCA export of a country Exports of capital goods of a country UN Comtrade (2019) BEC
Total exports of a country UN Comtrade (2019) BEC
Total exports of capital goods to the world UN Comtrade (2019) BEC
Total export to the world UN Comtrade (2019) BEC

There is, however, an additional interpretation that must be pointed out. A country can be a below-average exporter of capital goods. This does not mean that this country has not yet successfully adopted the related technology. It might mean that it uses the imported capital goods to produce other goods and it might even export them. Not every successful technology adopter becomes an exporter of capital goods. In simple terms: not all machines (and the technology embedded in them) are used to produce other machines. However, it will be shown in the analysis part of the module that different income groups of countries show distinct patterns respectively.

Once RCAs for imports and exports are calculated, they can be used to classify countries according to their technology adoption. Please keep in mind, that this is one way of reaching such a conclusion. It is, however, a good way as trade flows ‘reveal’ the behaviour of firms.

Countries will be classified according to their trade patterns, e.g. whether they are above or below average importers or exporters.

Box 3.1: Alternative identification and disaggregation

All shares that are discussed above could alternatively be calculated as shares in production by using nominal gross domestic product (GDP) in the denominator instead of total imports or exports. Nominal GDP is available from the World Bank’s ‘World Development Indicators’ (WDI 2019). The resulting indicator can act as a second benchmark when policy makers try to assess if they import more or less than comparator countries. If a country’s import share in general is higher than their peers, looking at imports of 4.0 goods in total imports could understate the expenditures in this product group.

Further, it should be clear that the indicators above can be applied to more disaggregate data, for example to specific goods, such as CAD-CAM or robotics. This would potentially reveal countries’ specialisation in very particular technologies or goods.

3.2 Robots and job loss in manufacturing

An important discussion with every new technological revolution is the question of whether it will substitute labour. One of the main public discussion is about job loss due to robot-based automation. Before an assessment can be made about potential risks of the newest technology, indicators that capture the use of robots, as well as how many people are potentially at risk of being replaced need to be introduced. To this end, the indicators described below have to be combined.

Box 3.2: Recommendation on aggregated employment measure

This section uses manufacturing employment at the aggregated sector level (for manufacturing as a whole) as well as employment figures on the industry-level within manufacturing following the ISIC rev. 3 classification. While we resort to INDSTAT (2019) data for the industry-level analysis, it is recommended to use ILO (2019) data when analysing the manufacturing-sector aggregate. In what follows, we introduce both data sources and illustrate that there are only marginal qualitative differences across both data sources.

3.2.1 Robot intensity

Robots are used as complements or substitutes for human labour. Instead of using absolute stocks, robot intensity is defined as the stock of robots per manufacturing employment. If this number is increasing, more robots are used for the same amount of human labour (or the same number of robots with less humans). In order to distinguish the two possible interpretations, the development of employment will be taken into account later.

Main question

  • How many industrial robots are used compared to manufacturing employees?

\[ Robot~intensity~within~manufacturing_{it} = \frac{Industrial~robot~operational~stock_{it}}{Manufacturing~employment_{it}} \] Data sources

Data sources for this indicator are the IFR (IFR 2019) for the stock of industrial robots used in all manufacturing industries and the ILO (2019) for employment in all manufacturing industries.

Table 3.9: Robot intensity in a country
Indicator Variable Source
Robot intensity within manufacturing Industrial robot operational stock used by all industries IFR (2019), World Robotics
Manufacturing employment ILO (2019) or UN INDSTAT (2019)

3.2.2 Growth rate of robot intensity

In order to be able to see, if the robot intensity increases or decreases over time, it can help to calculate annual growth rates.This number shows by how much robot intensity increases or decreases from one year to the next. It will be used it in the empirical analysis below to see that some countries show signs of maturity or saturation. That is, low annual growth rates indicating that the number of robots per worker does not increase significantly any more. This has to be taken into account in order to assess the risk of automation.

Main question

  • By how much did the robot intensity grow or shrink per year?

\[ Annual~growth~rate~of~robot~intensity_{it} = \frac{End~value_{it} - Initial~value_{it}}{Initial~value_{it}} \]

3.2.3 Robot intensity by industry

Robots are used in different industries to varying degrees. Some sectors use more, some very few robots compared to employees. In order to see, how countries use robots in different industries over time, robot intensity is calculated by industry.

Main question

  • How many industrial robots are used compared to manufacturing employees in different industries?

\[ Robot~intensity~within~manufacturing_{ijt} = \frac{Industrial~robot~operational~stock_{ijt}}{Manufacturing~employment_{ijt}} \]

Data sources

Data sources for this indicator are the IFR (IFR 2019) for the stock of industrial robots for a specific industry and the UN INDSTAT (2019) for employment of that industry.

3.2.4 Share of employees in risk industries

Now that indicators for the use of robots per employee are introduced, one can start to answer the question if and by how much workers are at risk of losing their job due to robot-based automation. Arguably, not every type of work is at the same risk of being replaced by a machine. Mayer (2018) has identified four manufacturing industries, in which this risk is particularly high. They are labelled ‘risk industries’ and they are Chemicals, Rubber and Plastic, Electronics as well as the automotive industry. Please look to Section 5.1.2 for a detailed discussion.

Main question

  • What is the share of workers in risk industries in total manufacturing employment?

\[ Share~of~employment~at~risk~in~total~manufac._{it} = \frac{Manufac.~employment~in~risk~industries_{ijt}}{Total~manufacturing~employment_{it}} \]

Data sources

Data sources (Table 3.11) is UN INDSTAT (2019) for sectoral employment and ILO (2019) for total manufacturing employment.

Indicator Variable Source
Share of employment at risk in total manufacturing Manufacturing employment in risk industries: electronics, automotive and rubber UN INDSTAT (2019)

Next, the share of workers in the manufacturing industries in total employment is calculated. This indicates one dimension of importance of the manufacturing sector for an economy. If the manufacturing sector as a whole is small, then automation might affect fewer people.

3.3 Productivity

The analysis of productivity related dynamics is built on two consecutive steps.

  • In Step 1 the three central variables of the analysis (productivity, employment, value added) are used to analyse industry-level growth dynamics and follow an inclusive and tenable growth trajectory (a positive growth rate of value added, employment and productivity). Throughout the analysis we refer to these industries as ‘runners’ (or runner industries).

  • In Step 2, the share of runner industries relative to the manufacturing sector of a country is assessed. This way the proportion of a country’s manufacturing industry characterized by positive productivity, value added and employment dynamics can be quantified.

See Table 3.13 for a list of relevant variables. Calculations in this section only resort to INDSTAT (2019) data.

Table 3.13: Structure of analysis
Indicator Variable
Step 1: Identification of runner industries Productivity CAGR
Employment CAGR
Value added CAGR
Step 2: Runner industry exposure Value added share of runner sub-sector
Employment share of runner sub-sector

3.3.1 Identification of runner industries

Strategic questions and data sources

  • What are the productivity growth dynamics of a particular industry and what do they depend on?
  • Are positive productivity growth rates always good?
  • Are there industries that provide positive growth and employment prospective even during the most recent periods of global market integration and fast-paced technological advancements?

The indicators used for this analysis are the sub-sector value added and employment series from INDSTAT (2019) both of which are used to calculate the third indicator (productivity) as described in Table 3.14.

Table 3.14: Data Sources for identification of inclusive and tenable industry growth
Indicator Variable Source
Employment growth rate Sub-sector employment-to-population compound annual growth rate (CAGR) UN INDSTAT (2019)
Value added growth rate Sub-sector value added per capita (VA) compound annual growth rate (CAGR) UN INDSTAT (2019)
Productivity growth rate Sub-sector productivity growth rate (CAGR) (calculated based on variables above)



Methodological underpinnings of runner identification analysis

The purpose of this step is to illustrate the effect of (changes in) productivity dynamics and how they relate to economy-wide performance patterns. The subsequent analysis is conducted on the basis of the compound annual growth rate (CAGR) which is calculated as

\[ CAGR = \frac{End~value}{Initial~value}^{1/no.~of~years} -1 \]

The CAGR corresponds to a smoothed annual average over the time horizon considered. It serves as a measure to illustrate how much a particular variable has grown over time. In the case of this tool, it is interesting to learn about the growth rates of employment, value added as well as productivity: Productivity is defined as the ratio of value added creation over the employment head count. In other words, it measures how much value added (in monetary terms) is produced by each employee of a respective industry in one year. Whereas this measure is interesting, the focus of this module is to assess the dynamics of productivity changes over time. This allows one to better assess the speed and direction of changes in the sectoral composition of the manufacturing industries of a country.

Please note that it is preferable to calculate productivity and value added growth rates based on real value added series. This is because nominal value added growth is a composition of the growth rates of real value added and that of prices, i.e. inflation. Calculating nominal value added growth rates could therefore result in unrealistically high (nominal) growth rates which are simply the result of high inflation. Obviously, this is not desirable. In Appendix E a simple and straightforward measure to calculate real value added series for manufacturing industries is proposed.

Based on this formula one can calculate CAGRs for productivity, value added and employment. As discussed in Section 2.2.4, some combinations of growth rates of these three variables are more desirable than others. Section ?? will focus on the analysis of industries that follow a positive value added as well as productivity growth, i.e. can be classified as runner industries.

3.3.2 Runner industry exposure

Strategic questions and data sources

  • What is the exposure of a country to industries that have experienced positive employment, value added and productivity growth in the past?
  • How does the share of these industries evolve over time?

The variables necessary to calculate runner industry exposure shares are listed in Table 3.15.

Table 3.15: Indicators for identification of exposure to runner industries
Indicator Variable Source
Runner value added share Manufacturing value added of runner sub-sector UN INDSTAT (2019)
Total manufacturing value added UN INDSTAT (2019)
Runner employment share Manufacturing employment of runner sub-sector UN INDSTAT (2019)
Total manufacturing employment UN INDSTAT (2019)

The shares for value added and employment are then calculated as

\[ Runner~value~added~share_{it} = \frac{Value~added~runner~industry_{it}}{Total~manufacturing~value~added_{it}} \] \[ Runner~employment~share_{it} = \frac{employment~runner~industry_{it}}{Total~manufacturing~employment_{it}} \]

In the two equations above \(Total~manufacturing~value~added_{it}\) and \(Total~manufacturing~employment_{it}\) refer to the sum of value added and/or employment over all available industries on the basis of the previously calculated data series. In other words, both denominators are not obtained from the pre-aggregated manufacturing sector series.
