Industry 4.0 and Productivity

I Progression patterns in import and export dynamics

While Figures I.2 and I.1 provide some interesting insights on the evolution of classification patterns of the different income groups over time, one can furthermore derive some clear empirical evidence in favour of a technological progression that is reflected in changes of the import/export structure of capital and industry 4.0 goods as economies growth richer. In this section we will take a closer look at the underlying fundamentals.

Remember that in in the section on technology adoption we have introduced a classification of the following sort:

RCA import RCA export Group
< 1 > 1 Specialized producer
> 1 > 1 Specialized producer and user
> 1 < 1 Specialized user
< 1 < 1 Non specialized

As it turns out, we can identify a strong regularity when comparing the classification patterns of capital goods vs. industry 4.0 goods: In 90 percent of recorded cases in our data, countries are in a ‘higher’ (more specialised) or the same group for capital goods than for industry 4.0 goods. In other words, they are ‘leading in capital goods but following in industry 4.0 goods’ in the sense that they have a higher proclivity to export capital goods before they export industry 4.0 goods. Table I.1 provides a more detailed account of that observation.32 There, for example, in 82 percent of cases a non specialised country in capital goods is also found to be non specialised in industry 4.0 goods. On the other hand, at least a third of specialized producers in capital goods are also specialized producers in industry 4.0 goods.

The above tables might be too much?

Figures 4.4 and 4.8 displayed average RCAs by income group. Averages were calculated for all years and per income group. A more detailed way to display these patterns is the following:

Figure I.1: Classification of countries by year and income group

This depiction shows shares of users and producers by income group across time. With this, one can find the following conclusions that go beyond simple means:

  • On average, low income countries are non-specialized in capital goods, neither importing nor exporting above average. Around 40% of them are specialized users, meaning they import and use some capital goods, starting the adoption process.
  • On average, lower middle income countries have higher shares of specialized users than low income countries and five to ten percent of them are also exporting capital goods above world average.
  • On average, higher middle income countries have a higher share of specialized users and producers compared to lower middle income countries and they already have some specialized producers.
  • On average, high income countries have the highest share of specialized producers and the lowest share of specialized users.

The same can be done for 4.0 goods and results in the following figure:

Figure I.2: Classification of countries by year and income group

Only high income countries are above average exporters of goods related to the newest wave of production technology. Additionally, the higher the income, the higher the share of countries that are specialized users, i.e. above average importers of modern production technology.

When we compare countries across groups of goods (capital goods vs. 4.0 goods), we find a very consistent pattern: the majority of countries (about 90%) are specialized producers and users in capital goods before they are in industry 4.0 goods. There are only a handful exceptions where a country would be classified as a specialized producer of the newest technology but not for capital goods. This should convince policy makers that a solid manufacturing basis that uses different vintages of technologies is a prerequisite for being a specialised producer in the most modern technologies. This pattern holds for virtually all low income countries as well as lower and upper-middle income countries in our sample. The only exceptions from this rule are some high-income countries that specialise in new 4.0 technology and are not main exporters of capital goods. They, however, most likely built a solid manufacturing basis in prior decades.
