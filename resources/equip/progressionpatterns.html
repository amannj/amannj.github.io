Industry 4.0 and Productivity

I Progression patterns in import and export dynamics

While Figure 4.8 and 4.4 provide some interesting insights on the evolution of classification patterns of the different income groups over time, one can furthermore derive some clear empirical evidence in favour of a technological progression that is reflected in changes of the import/export structure of capital and industry 4.0 goods as economies growth richer. In this section we will take a closer look at the underlying fundamentals.

Remember that in in the section on technology adoption we have introduced a classification of the following sort:

RCA import RCA export Group
< 1 > 1 Specialized producer
> 1 > 1 Specialized producer and user
> 1 < 1 Specialized user
< 1 < 1 Non specialized

As it turns out, we can identify a strong regularity when comparing the classification patterns of capital goods vs. industry 4.0 goods: In 90 percent of recorded cases in our data, countries are in a ‘higher’ (more specialised) or the same group for capital goods than for industry 4.0 goods. In other words, they are ‘leading in capital goods but following in industry 4.0 goods’ in the sense that they have a higher proclivity to export capital goods before they export industry 4.0 goods. Table I.1 provides a more detailed account of that observation.28 There, for example, in 82 percent of cases a non specialised country in capital goods is also found to be non specialised in industry 4.0 goods. On the other hand, at least a third of specialized producers in capital goods are also specialized producers in industry 4.0 goods.

The above tables might be too much?

Figure I.1 illustrates very clearly that, on average, there is a considerable spread for the export RCAs of capital goods: High income countries have on average the highest export RCAs, followed by upper middle, lower middle and finally low income countries. As for import RCAs, we can observe that, on average, the import propensity seems to first build up as economies grow richer but decline after a country has reached the lower middle income mark. It has to be noted, however, that for all four income group aggregates, the differences are both relatively small in terms of magnitude and also well centred around the global average mark of ‘1’.

Figure I.1: RCA exports and imports for capital goods by income group

Can a similar pattern be observed for industry 4.0 goods? As Figure I.2 reveals, the differences across income groups seem much more pronounced. While lower income countries are more rarely engaged in industry 4.0 goods trading activities, the import RCA for upper middle income countries is by far the highest while their corresponding export RCA is only slightly higher than that of its lower income counterparts. In other words: Upper middle income countries are predominantly importing industry 4.0 technologies. High income countries on the other hand are much more balanced. It has to be noted however, that among the group of high income countries there exists a small and particularly export-led sub-group of countries (see A) that are mainly responsible for driving up the average RCA export share of the high income countries. In that sense, the information provided in this section may be seen as evidence that the progression dynamics of industry 4.0 goods are currently unfolding within the group of high income countries while they have much more clearly emerged for capital goods and across all income groups already.
