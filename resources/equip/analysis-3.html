Industry 4.0 and Productivity

Section 6 Analysis 3

6.1 Productivity

This section links the current debate on technological revolution that is due to the emergence of smart digital technology to macroeconomic indicators across manufacturing industries. The instruments presented in the following paragraphs allow for a simple analysis of the differences in the dynamics of manufacturing industry’s productivity, value added and employment growth dynamics over time. It also proposes a simple way to analyse and compare country-specific industry profiles across aggregates and over time and furthermore defines a simple forward-looking approach to identify the direction into which a particular manufacturing industry is developing.

As discussed before, the concept of runner industries puts emphasis on the concept of sustained employment growth; a feature that cannot be overstated in light of the employment automation risk discussed in the context of the newly emerging technologies with smart systems and robots before. The concept of runner industries is also extended to that of runner industry exposure which constitutes a straightforward instrument to characterise the share of industries within manufacturing that feature positive productivity and employment characteristics. It is also very important to understand that the classification of runner industries, is derived from average global trends. Consequently, one should note that the idea of runner industries is not a static concept but fundamentally depends on the set of countries and time periods looked at. These implications will be discussed when talking about individual effects .

The subsequent paragraphs offer a case study runner industries analysis for Viet Nam and Morocco. It will further illustrate how policy analysts can utilise the instruments provided in this tool to understand the role as well as the aim and scope of the runner industry methodology.

6.1.1 Runner industries

The empirical analysis begins by identifying runner industries. Particularly through the adoption of new technologies, the importance of the identification of industries with the characteristics associated with positive value added, employment and productivity growth cannot be overstated:

This set of industries has remained at the forefront of value added and employment generation and therefore stands in stark contrast to the jobless or declining growth types discussed earlier; see Figure 2.5. In order to be able to identify the corresponding regime types associated with the respective industry, employment, value added as well as productivity compound annual growth rates (\(CAGR\)) are to be calculated using the following steps:

  • Retrieve data (see Table 3.14):
    • Obtain value added and employment data from INDSTAT.
    • Obtain population data from an alternative data source such as the World Development Indicators.
  • Calculate series:
    • Calculate value added per capita as well as the employment-population ratio.22
    • For the value added series it is necessary to eliminate any potential price effects in the series (see Appendix E for more details).
    • Calculate productivity series as23:

\[ Productivity = Value~added~per~capita / Employent~population~ratio \]

  • Calculate the compound annual growth rate (CAGR) for each of the three variables
    • Do this by calculating: \[ CAGR = \frac{End~value}{Initial~value}^{1/no.~of~years} -1 \]
  • Calculate averages of the CAGRs of value added, employment and productivities for the aggregates of interest. In our case we are interested in:
    • the global runners (taking averages of the growth rates across all countries);
    • the income group runners (averages across all countries belonging to the same income group as Viet Nam and Morocco, i.e. lower-middle income);
    • the runners for Viet Nam and Morocco exclusively.

The remainder of this section refers to global/income group and/Morocco and or Viet Nam runners as the set of runner industries that have been identified for each of the separate aggregates, respectively. The indicators obtained through this procedure form the basis of the subsequent analysis. Conducting the runner industry analysis as done above one can learn more about the general trend and dynamics within manufacturing. On the other hand, this approach also has its limitations: First of all, it analyses productivity growth over a very long period of time. At the same time, it creates averages across many countries, some of which may only have data for some earlier or later periods. In this sense, this initial analysis measures for example the effect of colour TVs in the 1970s together with the digital revolution as well as industry 4.0 together in the more recent past together. This can be both instructive but also misleading as particularly developing countries have much better data coverage for the most recent decades.

Consequently, if one would like to learn more about the runner industry trends in the more recent past, say, since 2000, a similar table can be obtained by simply truncating the data. The results are shown in Table 6.2 and show, that positive growth dynamics can now be reported for more manufacturing industries: Compared to before, now also the chemicals and precision instruments industries can be classified as runner industries. Please note that this difference of the patterns which are the result of changing the time dimension considered, are an important feature of the runner industry concept; see Appendix C.

It is important to note that the results discussed so far are only reflective of the global average and hence show the pooled experience across a very broad and heterogeneous set of countries, both of the developing and developed world. Since the very heterogeneous diffusion patterns of the new and digital technology in the previous sections became clear, it is also highly instructive to conduct the same runner industry identification for a more narrowly defined group of countries, such as for the group of countries belonging to the same income group (the so called ‘income group’ runners). Finally, the runner performance of reference countries Viet Nam and Morocco is interesting.25

A condensed summary of all classified runner industries across the different specifications is presented in Table 6.3. This tables shows that there are a great number of runner industries for lower income countries in the post-2000s where positive employment and productivity growth has been achieved despite the most recent technological developments. Table 6.3 also shows that runner industries are typically identified to be on average part of the medium- and high-technology group which are characterised by their higher propensity to undergo R&D endeavours (OECD 2011). Reasons why this should not be too big of a surprise will be discussed in the next paragraph. However, it is important that this is not the result of some fixed classification (as that of technology groups for example), but purely the result of the growth dynamics of value added and employment: If an industry follows these favourable growth dynamics, then it will also qualify as a runner industry. This can be seen for the food end beverages industry which, despite being considered a low-technology industry, also belongs to the set of runner industries in most of the cases. The latter is an important insight regarding future development as well: It could be possible, that new technologies will allow an industry that is currently low-technology, to become more technologically sophisticated in the future. If this goes hand-in-hand with an expansion of the industry (through value added and employment growth), it is possible to identify it as a runner industry. In this sense, the runner industry classification is capable of this kind of dynamism as well which can be referred to as intra-industry upgrading; see Appendix G for more information.

Looking at Table 6.3 it also makes sense to link the results back to the earlier discussion on new digital technology and high technology industries:

  • The first observation is that runner industries seem to be dominated by medium/high-technology industries: Following OECD (2011) the medium-/high-technology industries, chemicals, electrical machinery, precision instruments and motor vehicles very robustly appear across the different aggregates.

  • What is more, the high-risk industries (Mayer 2018) which have been identified earlier as being electrical machinery, motor vehicles as well as chemicals and rubber and plastic also find their way into this representation.

This overlap is both striking as it is probably not too much of a surprise: The industries that extensively undertake R&D are the ones that by and large are at the forefront of the current digital revolution in manufacturing. This might rightfully concern policy makers to ask to what degree jobs in these industries are at risk of being replaced by new and smart robots. At the same time these R&D-intensive industries have also been leading in the previous automation efforts in the past. Was this at the expense of jobs? As our runner analysis illustrates, it is precisely these industries that can be characterised by a positive growth of productivity, value added and employment. In essence, it is this set of industries that have had a shaping effect on the face of manufacturing: job destruction and job creation are central elements of the modernisation process of manufacturing industries.

Lastly, Table 6.3 also reveals that the runner profile for Viet Nam (as well as Morocco) is quite different to that of its lower middle income peers. At first sight it appears as if Viet Nam seems to report more runner industries for the low(er)-skilled manufacturing industries while the high(er)-skilled industries do not seem to qualify as runners. Explaining these differences is the goal of the next step.

6.1.2 Advanced manufacturing industry profiling

It is important to note that the idea of runner industries is not a static concept. It fundamentally depends on the set of countries and time periods looked at. In other words, even though one industry follows a declining growth trend on the global average, this does not imply that this will have negative value added and employment growth dynamics for all countries and regions, respectively. In the same vein, the observation that another industry has shown inclusive and tenable growth characteristics on the global scale does not imply that this industry must be equally successful across all countries considered. In this regard it may not be a sensible strategy for a developing country to identify and/or advocate for the development of runner industries as identified either on the global stage or, more specifically, with regards to a sub-set of advanced economies.

Having identified a successful runner industry in the aggregate does not imply that this industry will be equally successful across all countries. Consequently, for an analysis that is more focussed on the cross-sectional differences across countries and aggregates, it makes sense to summarise the growth rates of value added, employment as well as productivity in one big table similar to Table 6.4. Exemplarily, whereas the food and beverages industry has been following a declining growth trajectory it qualifies as a runner industry for Viet Nam. For the case of Morocco on the other hand, the food and beverage industry can be characterised by an employment-led growth trajectory. In other words, our analysis indicates that the Vietnamese and Moroccan food and beverage industries have followed a very different trend to that of the global average and/or that of its lower middle-income counterparts; they are also very different compared to each other.

Table 6.4 also allows us to investigate why the medium-/high-technology industries electrical machinery and precision instruments are not identified as runner industries for Viet Nam while they are identified for the income group runners: There is no data available for these industries for the case of Viet Nam. While this is certainly discouraging it does not mean that the analysis has to stop there: Inspecting the Vietnamese raw data reveals that there is no Index of Industrial Production (IIP) data available, a variable that is required to calculate the real value added series; see Box ?.? for more information. For a more detailed explanation on data availability, please consider Appendix E.

BOX ?.?: Recommendation on the use of IIP series in a situation of limited data coverage

In some cases it is not possible to derive meaningful statistics on the basis of the available data because of the lack of data. For example, if either value added or employment data is missing for a particular industry in question, a runner industry analysis would not be feasible anymore. In other cases Index of Industrial Production (IIP) necessary for the deflation of the nominal value added series is missing (see Appendix (???)(deflation) for more information) for a particular industry.

In this case it is recommended to check if the IIP series for the whole manufacturing aggregate are available. If so, the manufacturing sector IIP series can be used to deflate the nominal value added series for a particular industry instead. It has to be noted that in this case it is assumed, that the inflation dynamics of the whole manufacturing sector are similar to that of the particular industry of interest. While this may not necessarily be fully reflective of the actual industry level dynamics, it allows for a more comprehensive analysis of the industry dynamics within manufacturing which would have otherwise been impossible. In any case, analysts should always highlight it in their analysis if this ‘second best’ approach to deflating value added series is used.

Until now differences in the runner industries between the case of Viet Nam as well as Morocco and that of the aggregate of lower income countries have been identified. The next question is on how different the growth performance of Viet Nam is relative to that of all other lower income countries. This result is highlighted in Figure 6.1 which shows a simple histogram for the productivity, value added and employment CAGRs: This visual tool is helpful in illustrating how different the growth rates across the set of countries considered for the analysis is.

Figure 6.1: CAGR distribution food and beverages.

The red vertical lines identify the average growth rates for each of the three variables. Additionally, the three CAGRs for Viet Nam are highlighted in dark blue while the three CAGRs for Morocco are identified by the orange bars. This illustration serves two main purposes: First of all, it allows one to examine if the average growth rates are driven by some extreme (either very high or very low) growth rate or if they are representative of sector dynamics.26 In the case presented here the histogram seems to be somewhat positively skewed. This means that the right tail of the distribution (the right side of the histogram) is longer (has more higher values) than the left.27 In other words, there are a few countries reporting considerably higher growth rates for productivity, value added and employment. At the same time, the highest number of countries report productivity (value added) growth rates of roughly +2 (+1) percent and -0.5 percent for employment as illustrated by the peak of the histogram count. Secondly, by comparing the histogram and average with the recorded growth rates for Viet Nam (dark blue bar in Figure 6.1), one can easily and quickly compare Viet Nam’s growth performance relative to that of other countries: Whereas Viet Nam’s productivity growth rate appears to be in line with that of the majority of economies compared here, its value added and employment growth rates belong to the highest in the sample. This result again illustrates the importance of considering the productivity dynamics alongside that of its composing factors as a similar productivity growth rate could have also been achieved by a lower value added and potentially negative employment growth rate as the case of the lower middle income countries in Table 6.4 has illustrated. As for Morocco, Figure 6.1 illustrates that the Moroccan food and beverage industry has a value added growth rate close to that of the average while it also has one of the highest employment growth rates of all lower middle income countries. These two observations help understand why labour productivity for Morocco is amongst the lowest of all lower middle income countries considered.

6.1.3 Runner industry exposure

The previous steps discussed ways to measure the performance of manufacturing industries along the delineating lines of productivity, value added and employment. It was shown how this kind of analysis is sensible to the choice of country comparators and time dimensions. It also highlighted the importance of assessing an industry performance in the context of the variation across countries. While this may serve as an important step to learn about the dynamics of a particular (set of) industry(ies) of a country, it says very little about the characteristics of the manufacturing industry of a particular country as a whole. Consequently, and in order to assess the degree to which a country’s manufacturing sector is comprised of industries with a certain set of characteristics (like the ones described by the characterisation of runner industries), a simple ratio of runner industries in total manufacturing is proposed. To this end we use the term global runner exposure when evaluating the share of industries that have been identified as runners at the global stage. We use the same wording to refer to income group runners (income group runner exposure) and country-level runners (country-level runner exposure) when shares are calculated by using respectively runners identified at income group level and country level.

This way two simple indicators can be obtained that allow to assess the share of manufacturing industries in an economy that have experienced inclusive and sustained growth in the past. If, for example, the share of runner industries in total manufacturing of a country is very low, this may indicate higher risk of jobless growth in the wake of technological adoptions which would deserve attention from the policy makers especially in terms of measures for employment protection. Previous sections have also shown that in the long term a higher and prolonged exposure towards sectors which on average are not the best performers in terms of jobs creation may be associated with lower income levels. What is more, the concept of runner exposure may be useful not only in order to summarize the performance of one particular country, it can also be used to produce country rankings based on either the runner share in total manufacturing value added or employment, respectively.

The runner exposure is calculated on the basis of the country-level runner industries for Viet Nam and Morocco

and compared with share of runners in total value added identified at lower middle income countries level. As the presence of some missing values for runners especially in Viet Nam deriving from the lack of IIP data, shares of runners are calculated by using runners value added and total manufacturing value added by using only available data. However results are still very representative because the available data about sectors still represent about 85% and almost 100% of total value manufacturing value added in Morocco and Viet Nam. Results are presented in Table ??. Considerable differences between the country-level runner exposure analysis and its lower middle income counterpart in Table 6.6 can be seen: For example, the runner value added share increases from 33% to 88% and an even higher increase (from 25% to 89%) can be observed for the runner employment share for Viet Nam.

On the other hand, Morocco’s difference between the income group runner exposure and the country-level runner exposure is notably smaller. This is interesting for two reasons:

  • Morocco’s runner industry profile has more weight on medium- high-technology industries compared to Viet Nam as chemicals, machinery and precision instruments are part of Morocco’s runner industries but not Viet Nam’s.
  • Secondly, despite the fact that Morocco identifies 8 industries (compared to 7 for the case of Viet Nam) as runners, the corresponding runner industry shares for value added and employment is 16 to 26 percentage points lower than that of Viet Nam. This means that the share of industries with favourable productivity and employment trajectories are a much smaller part of the Moroccan manufacturing sector when compared to Viet Nam.

Despite some limitations in the coverage of real value added data the important take away policy implication from the table 6.5 is that country specific share of runners for both Viet Nam and Morocco is much higher than the share of runners calculated by using runners identified at lower middle income country level. This means that Viet Nam and Morocco are much more exposed towards runners sector compared to what expected on average and look more resilient to jobs displacing growth. Morocco is resilient to a less extent 9but in any case more than on average) as some low technology sectors (e.g. food and beverage and wearing apparel) which are traditionally preponderant at lower income levels are not identified as runners at country level.



SUMMARY

ADD MOROCCO

To conclude, this section has applied the concept of runner industries to identify growth dynamics. The example of the Vietnamese manufacturing industries is used and related to more general dynamics observed amongst the set of lower middle-income countries. The analysis has further highlighted the fact that the concept of runner industries is relative and therefore sensitive to the choice of data sample. Whereas this certainly makes the concept of runner industries a challenging instrument to apply, it also constitutes its biggest advantage over more static classifications: Following the notion that there is no ‘one-size-fits-all’ approach to manufacturing policy in general and technology adoption in particular, the runner industries framework allows for a flexible identification of growth enhancing identification and classification of industries. The instruments presented in this tool can be tailored and streamlined to follow the particular assessment needs of policy makers. At the same time, the runner instrument has also highlighted that even within a narrowly defined window of analysis, differences in the respective growth rates are reflective of country-specific effects and should therefore be given close consideration during the interpretation stage of the runner industry assessment. For a careful consideration of the runner industry classification in the case of Viet Nam it is important to contextualize the differences identified vis-à-vis the group of lower-middle income countries as well as the world trend. By and large Viet Nam can be characterized by reporting a disproportionally high contribution of employment-led industries (where positive employment growth out-paces value added growth thereby leading to a negative productivity growth rate). Vietnamese policy makers should be aware of these dynamics as they may bring about important ramifications particularly in the context of the adoption of new technologies.
